LAKE HOLON

Lake Holon, also known as Lake Maughan, is a crater lake located on Mt. Parker in South Cotabato, Philippines. It was formed after a violent volcanic eruption of Mt. Parker (also called Melibengoy by the Tboli people) in January 1641, one of the most powerful eruptions in Philippine history. The explosion created a vast crater that gradually filled with rainwater over the centuries, giving rise to the serene lake that exists today. For the indigenous Tboli tribe, the lake holds deep spiritual significance and is believed to be a sacred place guarded by ancestral spirits.


Today, Lake Holon is renowned as one of the cleanest inland bodies of water in the Philippines and a popular destination for eco-tourism and adventure seekers. It sits approximately 1,200 meters above sea level, surrounded by lush forests and mountain ridges that provide breathtaking views. The lake is home to various species of freshwater fish and is an important source of livelihood for nearby communities. Visitors can enjoy trekking, camping, kayaking, and cultural exchanges with the Tboli people, who continue to protect and preserve the natural beauty and cultural heritage of the area.


Lake Holon, also known as Lake Maughan, is a stunning crater lake located in the municipality of T’boli, South Cotabato, Philippines. It sits within Mount Parker, a dormant volcano, and is often referred to as the “Crown Jewel of the South” because of its pristine waters and breathtaking natural beauty. The lake is a popular destination for hikers, nature lovers, and eco-tourists who are drawn to its peaceful atmosphere and rich biodiversity. For the indigenous T’boli people, however, Lake Holon is much more than a tourist attraction—it is a sacred place deeply woven into their history, culture, and spiritual beliefs.


The T’boli people, who are the primary inhabitants of the area surrounding Lake Holon, are known for their strong connection to nature and their rich cultural traditions. They believe that the land, water, and forest are gifts from the spirits and ancestors that must be respected and cared for. Their way of life is guided by harmony with the environment, which is reflected in their customs, music, dances, and craftsmanship. The T’boli are famous for their T’nalak weaving, where designs come from dreams, symbolizing their deep spiritual connection to the unseen world.


According to T’boli beliefs, Lake Holon is inhabited by powerful guardian spirits who protect the area. They call the lake Bolon La S’bu, meaning “the spring source of abundance,” and consider it sacred. Local folklore tells that loud noises, disrespectful behavior, or pollution in the lake can anger the spirits, leading to bad weather or misfortune. Because of this, visitors are reminded to observe silence and respect when visiting the site. Rituals and offerings are often performed by elders or spiritual leaders to ask permission from the spirits before major activities, such as fishing, swimming, or even holding community events near the lake.


Music and dance play a vital role in the T’boli’s cultural expression around Lake Holon. They perform traditional dances like Kadal Tahaw (Dance of the Birds) and Madal Blelan (Dance of the Women), accompanied by indigenous instruments such as the hegalong (two-stringed lute), kulintang (gongs), and kubing (jaw harp). These performances are not only forms of entertainment but also expressions of gratitude and reverence to nature and their ancestors. Through these art forms, the T’boli pass down their stories, beliefs, and values from one generation to another.


Festivals around Lake Holon highlight both the natural and cultural heritage of the T’boli people. The most notable is the Holon Festival, which celebrates the lake’s beauty and promotes environmental protection and cultural pride. During this event, locals and tourists gather for rituals, traditional music and dance performances, and community feasts. The festival also aims to remind visitors of the importance of preserving the environment and respecting the customs of the indigenous people who call Lake Holon their home.


T’boli cuisine around Lake Holon reflects their resourcefulness and close relationship with nature. Their food is simple but flavorful, often made with ingredients found in the forests and fields. Common dishes include linagpang (grilled fish soup), native chicken cooked in coconut milk, and root crops such as cassava and sweet potatoes. Tilapia, caught from nearby lakes, is a staple, cooked in various ways such as grilled, fried, or made into chicharon na tilapia. Meals are usually shared by families and communities, symbolizing unity and generosity—values deeply rooted in T’boli tradition.


Today, Lake Holon continues to serve as a symbol of the T’boli people’s resilience, faith, and respect for nature. While it has become a major eco-tourism destination, the local government and the T’boli community work together to ensure that tourism remains sustainable and culturally sensitive. Visitors are encouraged to follow the community’s guidelines and participate in cultural immersion activities that promote understanding of the T’boli way of life. Through these efforts, Lake Holon remains not only a place of natural beauty but also a living testament to the T’boli’s enduring culture, beliefs, and harmony with the natural world.
