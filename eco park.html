THE ECO PARK

The Eco Park of Lebak was established as part of the local government’s initiative to promote environmental conservation and sustainable tourism. Originally, the area was a simple open space used by the community for small gatherings and recreational activities. Over time, it was developed into an eco-friendly park that highlights the natural beauty and biodiversity of Lebak, while also serving as a model for ecological awareness and green development. The project aimed to create a space where nature and community could thrive together, reflecting Lebak’s commitment to preserving its rich natural heritage.


Today, the Eco Park of Lebak serves as a popular destination for both locals and tourists who wish to enjoy outdoor leisure and environmental education. The park features walking trails, picnic areas, gardens, and spaces for cultural and recreational activities. It also promotes eco-friendly practices such as waste segregation, tree planting, and sustainable landscaping. Visitors can experience the tranquility of nature while learning about environmental stewardship, making it an ideal spot for family outings, school trips, and community events.

An eco park is a protected natural area designed to promote environmental conservation, sustainable tourism, and cultural preservation. It serves as a sanctuary for plants and animals while providing a peaceful place for people to relax, learn, and connect with nature. Many eco parks in the Philippines are located in areas rich in biodiversity, such as forests, mountains, and lakes. They are developed not only to attract tourists but also to encourage environmental awareness and respect for local traditions and communities. Eco parks often highlight the importance of protecting the environment while allowing visitors to experience its beauty responsibly.


The communities surrounding eco parks, especially in rural and indigenous areas, play a vital role in maintaining their natural and cultural balance. The local people serve as caretakers, guides, and storytellers, sharing their traditions and beliefs with visitors. Many of them belong to indigenous groups who have lived in harmony with nature for generations. Their way of life reflects a deep respect for the land, which they see as acred and life-giving. They believe that every tree, river, and mountain has a spirit that must be honored and protected. These cultural values help sustain the eco park and remind visitors to treat the environment with care and humility.


Beliefs and spiritual practices are an important part of life for these communities. Before starting any major activity, such as building, farming, or fishing, they often perform rituals to ask permission and blessings from the spirits of nature. Offerings like food, flowers, or native crafts are placed in sacred areas as a sign of gratitude and respect. In some eco parks, you may find prayer areas or ancestral grounds where local people continue to practice these customs. These traditions are passed down through storytelling, songs, and dances, ensuring that younger generations understand the importance of maintaining harmony with nature.


Culture in eco park communities is also expressed through art, music, and festivals that celebrate both nature and heritage. Visitors can experience traditional dances that mimic the movements of birds, rivers, or wind, as well as songs that tell stories of the people’s connection to their land. Handicrafts made from bamboo, abaca, and other natural materials are sold as souvenirs, showcasing the creativity and resourcefulness of local artisans. These cultural expressions not only preserve tradition but also provide livelihood opportunities for families, helping sustain both the culture and the eco park’s environment.


Food in eco park areas reflects the natural abundance of the surroundings. Meals are often made from locally grown crops, fresh vegetables, and river or lake fish. Traditional dishes may include linagpang (grilled fish soup), sinugba (grilled meat or fish), and laing (taro leaves cooked in coconut milk). Root crops like cassava and sweet potatoes are common staples, while fruits such as bananas and coconuts are used for snacks and desserts. Many eco parks also promote organic farming, encouraging the use of chemical-free ingredients. Sharing meals is considered an important part of community life, symbolizing unity, hospitality, and gratitude.


Overall, eco parks are more than just tourist destinations—they are living classrooms where people learn about the environment, culture, and sustainable living. They represent the connection between nature and humanity, where modern development and traditional beliefs can exist in harmony. The communities who live near these parks remind everyone that caring for the environment is not just an act of preservation but also a way of honoring the past and securing the future. Through the continued practice of their culture, beliefs, and food traditions, eco parks remain symbols of balance, beauty, and respect for the natural world.
