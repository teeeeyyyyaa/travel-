DINAGAT ISLAND

Dinagat Island, located in the Caraga region of the Philippines, was once part of the province of Surigao del Norte. It gained prominence during the 1960s when spiritual leader Ruben Edera Ecleo Sr. founded the Philippine Benevolent Missionaries Association (PBMA), which attracted a large following. The island’s people long sought independence, and on December 2, 2006, Dinagat Islands officially became a separate province through Republic Act No. 9355. Although its provincial status was briefly revoked in 2010 due to legal challenges, it was reinstated by the Supreme Court in 2012, reaffirming its identity as the Philippines’ newest province.


Dinagat Islands is an archipelagic province located off the northeastern coast of Mindanao, bordered by the Surigao Strait and the Philippine Sea. Its capital is San Jose, and the province is known for its rich natural resources, pristine beaches, limestone formations, and diverse marine life. Fishing, mining, and agriculture are the main sources of livelihood for its residents. The island is also home to unique flora and fauna, making it an emerging destination for eco-tourism and adventure seekers.


The cultural landscape of Dinagat Islands is a fascinating blend of traditional Filipino customs and unique local practices. The island's culture is heavily influenced by the PBMA movement, which has created a distinct spiritual community. The people of Dinagat, known as Dinagatnons, are known for their strong sense of community and environmental stewardship. Traditional arts like mat weaving using native materials, particularly the "tikug" grass, showcase the islanders' craftsmanship and connection to their natural surroundings.


Local beliefs in Dinagat Islands reflect a unique synthesis of Christianity, indigenous spirituality, and PBMA teachings. Many residents practice "panubad-tubad," a traditional healing ritual that uses locally gathered herbs and prayers. The island's numerous caves are considered sacred spaces, where elders perform "panawagtawag" ceremonies to commune with nature spirits. During the annual "Bugkos Festival," the community celebrates unity and thanksgiving through traditional dances, music, and spiritual gatherings.


The culinary heritage of Dinagat Islands features an abundance of seafood and locally grown ingredients. "Kinhason" (various shellfish) is a staple in local cuisine, often prepared in coconut milk with native herbs. "Tinolang Pakla," a soup made from grouper fish with local greens and ginger, is a beloved traditional dish. The islands are also known for "Ginamos," a fermented fish paste that adds a distinct flavor to many local dishes.


Local delicacies include "Binagoongang Hasil," made from tiny shrimp fermented with salt, and "Sinugbang Malonggong," grilled trumpet fish seasoned with native citrus and spices. The community takes pride in "Puto Dinagat," a special rice cake made with coconut milk and wrapped in banana leaves. During festivals, families prepare "Tinubdan," a traditional sweet made from native cassava and coconut, symbolizing the sweetness of community gatherings.


Marine harvesting practices are guided by traditional knowledge passed down through generations. Fishermen observe the lunar calendar for optimal fishing times and practice sustainable fishing methods. The preparation of "Dayok" (dried fish) and "Guinamos" (fermented fish paste) demonstrates the islanders' expertise in food preservation techniques that have sustained communities for generations.
