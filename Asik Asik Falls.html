Asik-Asik Falls

Asik-Asik Falls, located in Barangay Upper Dado, Alamada, North Cotabato, is a magnificent curtain-type waterfall that spans approximately 140 meters wide, cascading down a wall of green moss and ferns. The name "Asik-Asik" comes from the local Hiligaynon word meaning "to sprinkle" or "spray," perfectly describing how the water gracefully sprays down the rock face.


The cultural significance of Asik-Asik Falls runs deep among the Manobo tribe, the indigenous people who have long inhabited this region. The Manobos consider the falls a sacred site, believing it to be the dwelling place of their nature spirits called "Alabyog." According to their oral traditions, these spirits are the guardians of the waters and keepers of the forest's natural balance. The Manobo people have been the traditional stewards of this natural wonder for generations.


Local beliefs surrounding Asik-Asik Falls are rich with spiritual meaning. The Manobo people perform the "Panuvaran" ritual before entering the falls' vicinity, asking permission from the spirits for safe passage and blessings. They believe that the mist from the falls has healing properties, especially during the first light of dawn. The continuous flow of water is seen as a symbol of abundance and eternal life, and the locals believe that the falls' waters are purest during the full moon, when traditional healing rituals are often performed.


The culinary heritage of the area around Asik-Asik Falls reflects the bounty of the surrounding mountains and forests. The Manobo people are known for their traditional dishes such as "Inandap," a soup made with native herbs and freshwater fish found in the streams feeding the falls. "Binakbak," another local delicacy, consists of wild boar meat preserved through a traditional smoking process and flavored with local spices gathered from the forest.


Traditional food preparation in the area includes "Kakanin" or native rice cakes wrapped in banana leaves, often served during cultural ceremonies at the falls. "Sinagmay," a fermented rice wine, is produced locally and used in traditional rituals and celebrations. The community also harvests wild honey from the surrounding forest, which they believe carries the blessed essence of the falls' pristine environment. Seasonal fruits like "Marang" and "Durian" are abundant in the surrounding forests and form an important part of the local diet.


Today, while maintaining their cultural practices, the local community has embraced sustainable tourism, sharing their heritage with visitors while protecting the sanctity of the falls. Visitors are encouraged to respect local customs, such as offering a moment of silence before approaching the falls and refraining from loud noises that might disturb the spirits believed to dwell there.
