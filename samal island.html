SAMAL ISLAND

Samal Island, officially known as the Island Garden City of Samal, is part of Davao del Norte in the Philippines. Historically, the island was inhabited by the Sama-Bajau people, also known as sea gypsies or sea nomads. The name "Samal" is derived from these indigenous seafaring people who first called the island their home. Today, it's a thriving tourist destination known for its pristine beaches, rich marine life, and vibrant culture.


The cultural tapestry of Samal Island is woven from the traditions of its indigenous Sama-Bajau people and influenced by successive waves of settlers. The Sama-Bajau people traditionally lived in houseboats or stilt houses near the shore, reflecting their deep connection to the sea. Their traditional way of life revolves around the ocean, with fishing and diving being not just livelihood activities but cultural practices passed down through generations. The community's social structure is built around extended family units called "kumpania," which work together in fishing and other economic activities.


The spiritual beliefs of Samal's indigenous people are deeply rooted in animism and ancestor worship. They believe in "umboh," the spirits of their ancestors who guide and protect them at sea. Before any major fishing expedition, traditional rituals called "pagduwata" are performed to seek blessings and safe passage. The "igal," a traditional dance that mimics the movement of waves and sea creatures, is performed during important ceremonies and celebrations. The island also celebrates the "Kabasan Festival," honoring the bountiful harvest from the sea and showcasing traditional music, dance, and rituals.


Samal Island's cuisine is a reflection of its maritime heritage and abundant natural resources. Fresh seafood is at the heart of their traditional dishes, with specialties like "tiyula itum" (black soup made with burned coconut meat and tuna), and "piyanggang" (grilled fish in coconut milk and spices). The locals are known for their unique way of preparing "kinilaw" (similar to ceviche) using the freshest catch of the day, mixed with native vinegar, citrus, and local spices. "Tupig," a traditional rice cake wrapped in banana leaves, and "junay," a purple-colored rice cake made from native black rice, are popular local delicacies.


Traditional food preservation methods are still practiced on the island, including "daing" (sun-dried fish) and "guinamos" (fermented fish paste), which were essential survival techniques passed down through generations. During special occasions and festivals, communal cooking activities called "kanduli" bring the community together, where traditional dishes are prepared and shared. The preparation and sharing of food play a central role in strengthening community bonds and passing down cultural knowledge to younger generations.

After World War II, Samal’s communities gradually united, and in 1998, the island’s three municipalities—Babak, Samal, and Kaputian—were merged to form the Island Garden City of Samal (IGaCoS), now a thriving component city.


Today, Samal Island is one of Mindanao’s top tourist destinations, celebrated for its white-sand beaches, crystal-clear waters, and laid-back tropical charm. It lies just a short ferry ride from Davao City, making it easily accessible for both local and international travelers. The island offers a variety of attractions, including beach resorts, diving spots, waterfalls, and the famous Monfort Bat Sanctuary, home to the world’s largest colony of fruit bats. With its natural beauty and eco-friendly development, Samal continues to balance tourism growth with environmental conservation.
