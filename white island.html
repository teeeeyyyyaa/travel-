WHITE ISLAND

White Island, located off the coast of Mambajao in Camiguin, Philippines, is a natural sandbar that has existed for centuries. Unlike other islands, it has no vegetation or permanent structures and constantly changes shape depending on tides and weather conditions. Historically, it has been a favorite spot for locals and fishermen, serving as a resting point and a scenic backdrop to the majestic Mt. Hibok-Hibok. Over time, it became one of Camiguin’s most iconic attractions, drawing visitors for its pristine beauty and panoramic views.


White Island is an uninhabited white sandbar about 1.4 kilometers off the northern shore of Mambajao, accessible by a short boat ride from Barangay Yumbing or Agoho. The island offers clear turquoise waters ideal for swimming, snorkeling, and sunbathing. Its shape changes with the tide—sometimes forming a crescent, other times a horseshoe. With no shade or facilities, visitors are advised to bring sun protection and essentials. From White Island, tourists can enjoy a stunning 360-degree view of Camiguin, especially the nearby Mt. Hibok-Hibok and Mt. Vulcan.

The cultural heritage of White Island is deeply rooted in the maritime traditions of Camiguin's indigenous people. The local fishing community considers this sandbar a sacred site, believing it to be blessed by "Bantay Dagat" (sea guardians). These mystical guardians are said to protect both the waters and the fishermen who depend on them. The ever-changing shape of the sandbar is seen as a living symbol of nature's power and is treated with great respect by the local community.


Local folklore speaks of White Island's spiritual significance, particularly during special lunar phases. During full moons, traditional fishermen perform the "Pangamuyo sa Dagat" (prayer to the sea) ritual, offering small gifts of fruits, flowers, and local delicacies to honor the sea spirits. Many believe the pristine white sand has healing properties, especially at dawn when the first sunlight touches the shore. Elders tell stories of how their ancestors would collect small amounts of sand for traditional healing ceremonies.


The waters surrounding White Island are rich with marine life, contributing to the area's unique culinary traditions. Local fishermen regularly catch premium fish like "Tangigue" (Spanish mackerel) and "Tambakol" (yellowfin tuna). These fresh catches are often prepared as "Kinilaw," a local ceviche made with coconut vinegar, native citrus, and aromatic spices. The area is also famous for "Tuyom" (sea urchins), considered a delicacy and served fresh with local vinegar.


Traditional fishing families are known for their signature dish "Sinuglaw," which creatively combines grilled pork with fresh fish ceviche. The mainland community prepares "Binagol," a unique delicacy made from grated talipot palm pith and coconut milk, traditionally served in coconut shells. Local seaweed varieties like "lato" (sea grapes) and "guso" are harvested sustainably and prepared into refreshing salads with calamansi dressing.


Modern visitors to White Island are encouraged to respect these cultural traditions. Local boat operators maintain the custom of offering prayers before departure, and many share stories of the island's cultural significance with tourists. This balance of tourism and tradition helps preserve the spiritual and cultural heritage of this natural wonder for future generations.
