TINUY_AN FALLS

Tinuy-an Falls has a legend attached: it is said that people from Magdiwata Mountain were enslaved by tribes from Agusan. To free themselves, the enslaved plotted and eventually pushed their captors off a raft over the falls, thus gaining their freedom. This act was deliberate and purposeful, giving rise to the name Tinuy-an, which comes from a local word meaning “intentional act or performance to attain an objective or goal.


Tinuy-an Falls is a multi-tiered waterfall located in Bislig City, Surigao del Sur, Mindanao. It has three visible tiers (there’s a fourth that's hidden) and is very wide – often called the “Niagara Falls of the Philippines.” Visitors can swim in natural pools, trek to the different levels, and enjoy the view. Entrance is affordable (about PHP 50 per person), with extra fees for raft rides and life vest rentals.

Tinuy-an Falls, often called the “Niagara Falls of the Philippines,” is one of the most beautiful waterfalls in the country. It is located in Barangay Borboanan, Bislig City, Surigao del Sur, in the island of Mindanao. The falls measure about 95 meters wide and 55 meters high, making it the widest waterfall in the Philippines. Surrounded by lush forests and clean waters, Tinuy-an Falls is not only a natural wonder but also a symbol of the rich culture and traditions of the people living in the area.


The falls attract both local and foreign tourists who are amazed by its three-tiered cascades. The mist from the falls creates rainbows almost every morning, giving visitors a magical experience. Tourists can enjoy swimming, bamboo rafting, and taking pictures of the breathtaking scenery. The people of Bislig are known for being warm and hospitable, always ready to welcome visitors and share stories about the place’s history and significance.


The culture surrounding Tinuy-an Falls reflects the diverse heritage of the Mindanao people, especially the Mandaya and Manobo tribes. These indigenous groups have lived in the area for generations, preserving their traditional dances, songs, and handicrafts. They celebrate their customs through colorful festivals that showcase their respect for nature, their ancestors, and their community. Visiting Tinuy-an Falls gives tourists a glimpse into the peaceful and respectful way of life of these native tribes.


Local legends say that Tinuy-an Falls was once a place of freedom for enslaved natives. According to the story, the slaves rebelled against their cruel master and drove him away, symbolizing their victory and independence. The name “Tinuy-an” comes from the Visayan word meaning “intentional act” or “deliberate effort,” reflecting the courage and unity of the people. Many locals also believe that the spirits of nature protect the area, so they offer prayers and small rituals before entering the falls to show respect.


The nearby communities around Tinuy-an Falls also offer a variety of traditional foods that reflect the rich culture of Surigao del Sur. Popular dishes include kinilaw (fresh raw fish marinated in vinegar and spices), sinugba (grilled fish or meat), and puso (rice wrapped in coconut leaves). Visitors can also try kakanin such as biko and suman, which are sweet rice cakes made from sticky rice and coconut milk. These dishes are often served during gatherings, fiestas, and celebrations near the falls.


Tinuy-an Falls plays a vital role in the local economy and cultural preservation of Bislig. Tourism provides jobs to residents, from tour guides and food vendors to souvenir makers. At the same time, the cultural heritage of the indigenous people is promoted and respected. The local government and community members work together to maintain the beauty and cleanliness of the falls while protecting the traditions that make the place unique.


Tinuy-an Falls is more than just a tourist destination—it is a living symbol of nature, culture, and history. Its magnificent beauty represents the harmony between people and the environment. The beliefs, foods, and traditions of the locals make every visit meaningful, reminding everyone that preserving culture is just as important as protecting nature. Truly, Tinuy-an Falls stands as a precious treasure of Surigao del Sur and the entire Philippines.
